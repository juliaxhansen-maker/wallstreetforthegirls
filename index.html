The Exorbitant Privilege, Explained Properly.

There's a special prize that comes with printing the money the entire world wants to hold. Because everyone needs dollars — to trade, to hold in reserve, to feel safe — the US borrows more cheaply than anyone else on earth.

But that privilege isn't a law of nature. It's a popularity contest that has to be won again every single year. This deep dive traces where the phrase came from, how the advantage actually works in practice, and what history says happens when a reserve currency starts being re-examined.

Market Takes

Short notes between issues.

Quick reads for when something in markets deserves a comment but not a whole issue. Three or four paragraphs, no more — promise.

July 1, 2026

Gold keeps hitting records, and nobody's panicking.

Usually gold at all-time highs means fear is in the room. This time it's quieter than that — central banks are buying steadily and deliberately, the way you build an emergency fund, not the way you panic-shop. When the calmest money in the world keeps adding the same asset month after month, that's not a mood. That's a plan. Worth watching what they do next, not what the headlines feel like.

June 24, 2026

The "higher for longer" era is aging out of the conversation.

For two years, every rate conversation ended the same way: higher for longer. Lately the phrase is fading from central bank speeches — not because rates dropped, but because everyone has finally adjusted to them. Markets don't react to conditions; they react to surprises. Once something stops being surprising, it stops moving prices. The takeaway: the next big move comes from whatever we're not used to yet.

June 17, 2026

Earnings season is a vibe check, not a report card.

Companies can post record profits and still watch their stock fall — because markets grade against expectations, not against last year. If everyone expected an A+ and you delivered an A, that's a miss. It sounds unfair until you realize prices had already celebrated the A+ months in advance. The number that moves a stock isn't the result. It's the distance between the result and what everyone had quietly assumed.

About the newsletter

Finance was never meant to be a members-only club.

Wall Street for the Girls started with a simple frustration: financial news is written for people who already understand it. Every issue takes one big story from the markets and walks through it the way a friend would — with analogies that actually land, context that actually helps, and zero assumptions about what you "should" already know.

Each week covers the signal versus the noise, what's moving in markets, how it all connects, and — most importantly — why it matters to you, whether you're building your financial literacy from scratch or managing a portfolio.

Because understanding money is how you make confident decisions with it. Compound interest, compound confidence.
